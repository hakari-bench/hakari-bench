# NanoBEIR-en / NanoSCIDOCS

## Overview

SCIDOCS is a scientific-document representation benchmark introduced with
SPECTER. `NanoSCIDOCS` is the compact English NanoBEIR retrieval version: each
query is the title of a scientific paper, and relevant documents are related
scientific papers represented by title-and-abstract text. The task tests whether
a retriever can rank papers that are academically related, not just papers that
share the same keywords. It is closest to citation-informed paper discovery,
scientific recommendation, and related-work retrieval.

## Details

### What the Original Data Measures

[SPECTER: Document-level Representation Learning using Citation-informed
Transformers](https://arxiv.org/abs/2004.07180) introduces SPECTER and the
SCIDOCS evaluation suite. The paper argues that title and abstract text are rich
but insufficient by themselves for scientific document embeddings unless the
model also learns inter-document relatedness. SPECTER therefore uses citation
links as training signal and evaluates on SCIDOCS, a suite of seven
document-level tasks covering citation prediction, user activity, document
classification, and recommendation.

The SCIDOCS design matters because this task is not ordinary ad hoc search. The
query is not a user question; it is a paper title. A relevant document may be a
paper that shares a citation neighborhood, topic, methodology, user browsing
context, or recommendation signal. [BEIR: A Heterogeneous Benchmark for
Zero-shot Evaluation of Information Retrieval Models](https://arxiv.org/abs/2104.08663)
uses SCIDOCS in its Citation-Prediction group, where short scientific paper
titles retrieve scientific documents. In the NanoBEIR version, all positives are
binary qrels, but the underlying relation should be read as scholarly
relatedness rather than answer containment.

### Observed Data Profile

The sampled Nano task has 50 queries, 2,210 documents, and 244 positive qrel
rows. Every query has multiple positives: the average is 4.88 positives per
query, with a minimum of 3 and a maximum of 5. Queries are paper titles with an
average length of 72.78 characters. Documents are scientific abstracts or
title-plus-abstract records with an average length of 1,093.83 characters.

The query topics span computer science, information systems, networking,
business, education, computer vision, reinforcement learning, wireless systems,
security, and data mining. Examples include `Learning Actionable Representations
with Goal-Conditioned Policies`, `Fast Sparse Gaussian Markov Random Fields
Learning Based on Cholesky Factorization`, and `Question Answering in the
Context of Stories Generated by Computers`. Positive documents are often
methodologically or topically related rather than simple paraphrases. For
example, the goal-conditioned policy query links to reinforcement learning,
pixels-to-torques, and deep representation papers; the inter-transaction
association-rule query links to database mining algorithms.

The sampled corpus also contains visible artifacts from scholarly metadata
sources, including records with very short text and some copyright or archive
notices. These are part of the retrieval environment and make the task messier:
a model must handle scientific abstracts when present, but also avoid
over-ranking boilerplate and sparse metadata records.

### BM25 Difficulty

Using the dataset-provided BM25 candidate column, BM25 reaches nDCG@10 = 0.3360
and hit@10 = 0.7800. BM25 places 22 positive rows at rank 1, but the median
positive rank is 33 and only 83 of 244 positive qrel rows appear in the top 10.
The hit rate is reasonable because each query has several positives, while the
nDCG score shows that BM25 often fails to rank the whole related-paper set near
the top.

BM25 works when the related papers reuse specialized terminology. For `Decision
trees for predicting the academic success of students`, a positive about data
mining for student academic success is rank 2. For `Breaking the Barrier of
Transactions: Mining Inter-Transaction Association Rules`, a positive about
association-rule discovery is rank 5. For `Smart antennas for satellite
communications on the move`, positives about Ka-band satcom antennas appear at
ranks 5 and 20.

The harder cases show the difference between text overlap and scholarly
relatedness. For `Bank distress in the news: Describing events through deep
learning`, several positives are about distributional semantics or information
retrieval rather than bank distress, so lexical retrieval is distracted by news
and event words. For `Learning to Rank Non-Factoid Answers: Comment Selection in
Web Forums`, positives include neural matching and sequence models, while BM25
first retrieves generic QA passages. For `Enablers and Barriers to the
Organizational Adoption of Sustainable Business Practices`, BM25 finds documents
sharing `barriers`, `adoption`, or `sustainability`, but the positive set
includes broader information-systems and business-practice papers. Strong models
need scientific-document representations that capture method, application area,
and citation-like context.

### Training Data That May Help

Useful training data includes non-overlapping scientific citation pairs,
co-citation pairs, related-paper recommendation logs, Semantic Scholar-style
title/abstract/citation triples, and hard negatives from nearby papers in the
same venue or topic. The SPECTER training setup is especially relevant because
it trains on query, positive citation, and negative paper triples. Evaluation
queries, qrels, and candidate records from SCIDOCS or BEIR/NanoBEIR should be
excluded when they may overlap with this task.

Generic web retrieval or QA data is less directly useful. The model needs to
learn document-level academic relatedness from title and abstract text, including
method names, task names, research fields, and citation-neighborhood cues.

### Synthetic Data Guidance

For document-to-query generation, use non-evaluation scientific abstracts and
generate plausible paper titles, related-work titles, or citation-intent labels.
Synthetic positives should be papers that share a method, dataset, task,
application domain, or citation role, while hard negatives should share keywords
but differ in research contribution.

For joint generation, create clusters of scientific paper titles and abstracts
around a research topic, then mark several related papers per query. The
generated records should preserve abstract style, method names, dataset names,
metrics, and domain terminology. Do not seed generation with Nano evaluation
queries or positive documents. Multi-positive training, listwise losses, or
distillation from a strong scholarly recommender are more appropriate than
single-positive contrastive pairs.
