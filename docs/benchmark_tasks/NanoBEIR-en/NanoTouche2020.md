## Overview

Touché 2020 is an argument retrieval benchmark for controversial and
decision-oriented questions. `NanoTouche2020` is the compact English NanoBEIR
version of the conversational argument retrieval task: queries are short
controversial questions, and relevant documents are argumentative passages that
address the topic. The task tests whether a retriever can find diverse pro and
con arguments from debate-style text, not just web pages or answer snippets that
repeat the query words.

## Details

### What the Original Data Measures

[Overview of Touché 2020: Argument
Retrieval](https://downloads.webis.de/touche/publications/papers/bondarenko_2020d.pdf)
describes the first Touché lab at CLEF 2020. The lab ran two tasks: retrieving
arguments on socially important topics and retrieving argumentative documents for
everyday comparative decisions. The NanoBEIR `NanoTouche2020` task corresponds
to the first setting, where systems retrieve arguments from a focused crawl of
online debate portals for a controversial topic.

The paper states that Task 1 used 50 TREC-style search scenarios with a title,
description, and narrative. The document collection was based on the args.me
corpus, containing roughly 400,000 arguments crawled from debatewise.org,
idebate.org, debatepedia.org, and debate.org. Human assessors judged whether
retrieved texts were arguments and rated relevance on a graded scale. This means
the retrieval target is not a single factual answer or a direct counterargument
pair. A good result is an argument that substantively addresses the controversial
question, possibly from either side.

[BEIR: A Heterogeneous Benchmark for Zero-shot Evaluation of Information
Retrieval Models](https://arxiv.org/abs/2104.08663) includes Touché-2020 as an
Argument Retrieval dataset with short controversial queries and long
argumentative documents. The NanoBEIR version exposes binary positive qrels for
compact evaluation, but the original task context is graded argument relevance.

### Observed Data Profile

The sampled Nano task has 49 queries, 5,745 documents, and 932 positive qrel
rows. Every query has multiple positives: the average is 19.02 positives per
query, with a minimum of 6 and a maximum of 32. Query text is short, averaging
43.43 characters, and usually follows a yes/no controversial question pattern:
`Should gay marriage be legal?`, `Is homework beneficial?`, `Should felons who
have completed their sentence be allowed to vote?`, and `Should performance-
enhancing drugs be accepted in sports?`

Documents are much longer, averaging 2,142.57 characters, and often come from
online debate rounds. They include claims, rebuttals, citations, informal
language, spelling errors, quotations, and sometimes whole multi-point debate
responses. The positive sets are intentionally broad. For `Should euthanasia or
physician-assisted suicide be legal?`, positives include both arguments for
patient choice and arguments that frame euthanasia as morally wrong. For `Do
violent video games contribute to youth violence?`, positives include short
informal comments as well as longer claims about parental responsibility and
media effects.

This makes the task different from ArguAna. ArguAna asks for the best paired
counterargument to a long argument. Touché asks for many relevant arguments for a
short controversial topic. Stance diversity, topical coverage, argument quality,
and robustness to noisy debate text all matter.

### BM25 Difficulty

Using the dataset-provided BM25 candidate column, BM25 reaches nDCG@10 = 0.7072
and hit@10 = 1.0000. Every query has at least one positive in the top 10, which
is expected because the queries are short topic titles and many relevant
arguments repeat the same key terms: `abortion`, `minimum wage`, `death
penalty`, `homework`, `marijuana`, or `bottled water`.

The more meaningful signal is how many positives are ranked high. Across 932
positive qrel rows, only 318 appear in the top 10, and the median positive rank
is 22. For `Should abortion be legal?`, positives are spread across ranks 6, 7,
9, 11, 12, 17, and much lower. For `Should animals be used for scientific or
commercial testing?`, relevant arguments appear at ranks 4, 5, 8, and 9, but
also much lower in the candidate list. For `Should the federal minimum wage be
increased?`, BM25 finds several high-quality lexical matches but still spreads
many positives through ranks beyond 20.

This means hit@10 is too forgiving for this task. A useful retriever should rank
a set of distinct, relevant arguments high enough to support debate exploration.
It should also avoid over-focusing on a single stance or wording pattern when the
original topic narrative expects relevant pro and con arguments.

### Training Data That May Help

Helpful training data includes non-overlapping debate-portal arguments,
argument-relevance judgments, pro/con topic retrieval data, stance-labeled
argument pairs, and query-to-argument supervision from args.me-like corpora.
Touché, args.me, or BEIR-derived records likely to overlap with Nano evaluation
topics or documents should be excluded from training.

Training should include multi-positive or listwise objectives, since each topic
has many acceptable arguments. Hard negatives should be debate texts that mention
the same topic but are not relevant under the topic narrative, plus arguments
that are relevant to adjacent topics but do not answer the query.

### Synthetic Data Guidance

For document-to-query generation, use non-evaluation debate arguments and
generate short controversial questions that the argument addresses. Synthetic
queries should cover policy, ethics, education, health, law, environment,
technology, and social issues, and should support both pro and con arguments.

For joint generation, create topic clusters with a controversial question,
several stance-diverse argumentative passages, and same-topic non-relevant or
low-quality negatives. Generated arguments should include claims, premises,
evidence, rebuttals, and informal debate style. Do not seed generation with Nano
evaluation queries or positive documents.
