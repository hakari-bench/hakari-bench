## Overview

Natural Questions is an English open-domain question answering benchmark built
from real Google search queries and Wikipedia evidence pages. `NanoNQ` is the
compact English NanoBEIR retrieval version of the task: each query is a natural
information-seeking question, and the relevant documents are Wikipedia passages
that contain the answer-bearing evidence. The task tests whether a retriever can
connect everyday user questions to the right encyclopedic passage, not merely to
a page with overlapping words.

## Details

### What the Original Data Measures

[Natural Questions: A Benchmark for Question Answering
Research](https://aclanthology.org/Q19-1026/) introduces NQ as a QA dataset
whose questions are real anonymized, aggregated Google queries. In the original
task, annotators see a question and a Wikipedia page from the top five Google
search results, then mark a long answer, usually a paragraph or table region,
and a short answer when one is present. The paper emphasizes that NQ was designed
around naturally occurring questions, answer variability, and end-to-end
question answering rather than questions written after reading a paragraph.

The source paper matters for interpreting the retrieval task because the query
is not a synthetic cloze or a crowd-written reading-comprehension question. It is
often a lowercase, direct search query such as `who played bat masterson in the
tv series` or `where was the 3/5 compromise originally stated in the
constitution`. [BEIR: A Heterogeneous Benchmark for Zero-shot Evaluation of
Information Retrieval Models](https://arxiv.org/abs/2104.08663) includes NQ in
its Question Answering group and reports it as a binary-relevance Wikipedia
retrieval dataset with short questions and paragraph-like documents. BEIR/NanoBEIR
therefore turns the original answer selection problem into first-stage retrieval:
find the passage that contains the long-answer evidence.

### Observed Data Profile

The sampled Nano task has 50 queries, 5,035 documents, and 57 positive qrel rows.
Most queries have exactly one positive passage; seven queries have two positives.
The average query length is 47.04 characters, and the average document length is
525.60 characters. The queries are all natural-language information needs with
lowercase search-query style: `who`, `where`, `when`, `what`, `how`, and `why`
questions dominate.

The visible data covers pop culture, history, law, biology, geography, sports,
technology, and factual definitions. Positive documents are Wikipedia passages
that usually include enough context to answer the question, such as the location
of the Three-Fifths Compromise in the Constitution, Gene Barry playing Bat
Masterson, or Real Madrid winning the 2016 UEFA Champions League Final. Some
queries require entity grounding plus a specific relation: `why is the angel of
the north there` needs the passage explaining the statue's significance, and
`who was an early advocate for using scientific methods based on inductive
reasoning` needs passages about scientific method rather than generic inductive
logic.

The task is less multi-hop than HotpotQA and less paraphrase-oriented than Quora.
It is a fact/evidence retrieval task over Wikipedia passages. A strong model
should preserve rare entities and dates, but it also needs to understand the
question relation: location, actor, origin, office, cause, definition, winner, or
date.

### BM25 Difficulty

Using the dataset-provided BM25 candidate column, BM25 reaches nDCG@10 = 0.4708
and hit@10 = 0.6800. BM25 ranks 14 positives first, and 35 of 57 positive qrel
rows appear in the top 10. This makes the baseline moderate: lexical matching is
often enough when the question contains a distinctive entity, but many questions
still need relation-level evidence selection.

The successful cases are straightforward lexical/entity matches. For `who laid
foundation stone of islamia college peshawar`, the positive passage about Haji
Sahib of Turangzai is rank 1. `who played bat masterson in the tv series` has
the positive at rank 2, and `who won the champions league final in 2016` has the
positive at rank 3. In these cases, the rare entity phrases mostly identify the
right page and passage.

The failures show why the task is not just entity lookup. For `why is the angel
of the north there`, BM25 first finds an image-caption-like passage and unrelated
angel content, while the positive passage explaining the statue's coal-mining,
industrial-transition, and symbolic significance is rank 69. For `where was the
diary of a wimpy kid filmed`, BM25 finds film-series and book passages before
the positive passage about production locations in Vancouver and Coquitlam. For
`who was the highest ranking official in the early christian church`, BM25 is
distracted by generic early Church and modern church-leadership passages before
the positive passage about the pope. These examples show that models can improve
over BM25 by ranking passages according to the requested relation, not just the
topic.

### Training Data That May Help

The original Natural Questions training split is the first source to inspect for
question-to-Wikipedia evidence supervision, but upstream development/test data
or BEIR/NanoBEIR evaluation records likely to overlap with this task should
preferably be excluded. Other useful sources include open-domain QA retrieval
pairs, Wikipedia passage retrieval with answer spans, KILT-style question-to-page
supervision, and non-overlapping NQ-derived training data where the positive
passage contains the answer.

Training should emphasize question-to-evidence matching rather than direct
answer generation. Learning only question-answer pairs can help with entity
knowledge, but retrieval models need positive passages and hard negatives from
nearby Wikipedia pages or same-page paragraphs.

### Synthetic Data Guidance

For document-to-question generation, use non-evaluation Wikipedia passages and
generate natural search-style questions answerable from a single passage. Good
synthetic questions should vary relation type: `who`, `where`, `when`, `what`,
`how many`, `why`, definition questions, film or sports facts, historical office
questions, and scientific terms.

For joint document-and-question generation, create Wikipedia-style paragraphs
with entities, dates, roles, locations, and concise factual statements, then
write questions whose answers require selecting the right passage. Do not seed
generation with Nano evaluation queries or positive passages. Hard negatives
should share the same entity or broad topic while lacking the requested relation.
