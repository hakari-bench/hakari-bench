## Overview

FEVER is a fact-verification benchmark where textual claims must be checked
against evidence from Wikipedia. `NanoFEVER` is the compact English NanoBEIR
retrieval task: each query is a short declarative claim, and the system must
retrieve Wikipedia evidence documents that support or refute it. The task tests
claim-to-evidence retrieval rather than question answering. A strong retriever
must use named entities, dates, attributes, relations, and contradiction cues to
surface evidence even when the claim itself is false.

## Details

### What the Original Data Measures

[FEVER: a Large-scale Dataset for Fact Extraction and
VERification](https://arxiv.org/abs/1803.05355) introduces FEVER as a benchmark
for verifying textual claims against textual sources. The paper reports 185,445
claims generated from Wikipedia and then manually verified as `SUPPORTED`,
`REFUTED`, or `NOT ENOUGH INFO`. For supported and refuted claims, annotators
also record the sentence or sentences needed as evidence.

The paper stresses that FEVER differs from textual entailment because the
evidence is not given to the system. It must be retrieved from a large Wikipedia
collection before verification can happen. It also differs from conventional
question answering because a claim may omit the very information needed to find
refuting evidence. The paper's example, "Fiji's largest island is Kauai", can be
refuted only by retrieving evidence about Kauai and Fiji rather than by simply
answering a question.

FEVER claims were created in two stages. First, annotators wrote claims from
sentences sampled from introductory sections of roughly 50,000 popular Wikipedia
pages. They also created mutations through paraphrasing, negation, entity or
relation substitution, and making the claim more general or more specific.
Second, separate annotators labeled each claim and selected evidence from
Wikipedia. The paper reports that more than one evidence sentence was considered
appropriate in 31.75% of claims, 16.82% required evidence composition from
multiple sentences, and 12.15% used evidence from multiple pages. These details
matter because FEVER retrieval is not just title lookup; it is the first stage
of a larger evidence-grounded verification pipeline.

[BEIR: A Heterogeneous Benchmark for Zero-shot Evaluation of Information
Retrieval Models](https://arxiv.org/abs/2104.08663) includes FEVER in the
fact-checking group. In the BEIR/NanoBEIR retrieval setting, the claim is the
query and the retriever is evaluated on whether it ranks the evidence-bearing
Wikipedia documents high enough for downstream verification.

### Observed Data Profile

The sampled Nano task has 50 queries, 4,996 documents, and 57 positive qrel
rows. Most queries have one positive evidence document, but six queries have
multiple positives and the maximum is three. The average query length is 45.42
characters, while the average document length is 1,228.71 characters. The query
side is therefore compact, but the documents are longer Wikipedia-style
passages.

The queries are not natural search questions. They are declarative claims such
as `Timur was an Arabian lord.`, `Commodore is only a land rank.`, `Wolfgang
Amadeus Mozart was a wrestler.`, or `Secret and technologically advanced
airplanes were produced in Burbank, California.` Many claims are false or
partially false, so a retriever must not treat the claim wording as a fact. It
must retrieve pages that allow a verifier to decide whether the claim is
supported or refuted.

The documents are Wikipedia passages centered on entities, events, works, ranks,
or places. Positives often contain the crucial correction: Timur is described as
a Turco-Mongol conqueror rather than an Arabian lord; Commodore is described as
a naval rank; Mozart is described as a composer; and a page about a film titled
`The Paper` refutes the year 1894 by stating that it is a 1994 film. This makes
the task entity-heavy, but the evidence relation is different from generic
entity retrieval because the positive document must explain the truth condition
of the claim.

### BM25 Difficulty

Using the dataset-provided BM25 candidate column, BM25 reaches nDCG@10 = 0.8141
and hit@10 = 0.9400. This is a strong sparse baseline: many claims include rare
entity names or titles that appear directly in the relevant Wikipedia page.
BM25 ranks a positive first for 36 of the 50 queries, and 47 of 50 queries have
a positive in the top 10.

The remaining failures show where lexical matching breaks down. For `The Paper
was only released in 1894.`, BM25 is distracted by generic pages about paper and
by people born in 1894; the relevant evidence is the 1994 film page. For
`Wolfgang Amadeus Mozart was a wrestler.`, BM25 retrieves Mozart-related works
and family pages before the core Mozart biography. For `Woodrow Wilson lived
during The Russo-Persian War.`, the query requires relating a person's lifetime
to the date of a historical war, but BM25 first returns other Woodrow Wilson
pages and artifacts.

These cases show that the task is easy when the entity title dominates the
query, but harder when the decisive evidence is a date, profession, nationality,
rank type, or relation. A retriever that only rewards lexical overlap may rank
nearby pages about the same entity family, franchise, or title above the page
that actually verifies the claim.

### Training Data That May Help

Useful non-synthetic training data includes FEVER training claims with evidence,
other claim-verification datasets with evidence annotations, Wikipedia
claim-evidence pairs, and supervised retrieval data where the query is a
declarative statement rather than a question. Natural language inference data
can help with support/refute semantics, but it is not sufficient by itself
because FEVER retrieval requires finding evidence before making the verification
decision.

Training should preferably exclude upstream dev/test records, BEIR-derived
evaluation records, and any claim/evidence pairs likely to overlap with the
NanoBEIR evaluation split. The most helpful data will teach the model to map a
claim to the page that verifies the decisive entity, date, occupation, relation,
or location, not only to the most lexically similar page title.

### Synthetic Data Guidance

For document-to-question generation, start from non-evaluation Wikipedia
passages and generate short declarative claims that can be supported or refuted
by those passages. The claims should include named entities, dates, titles,
nationalities, professions, locations, and simple relations. Include both true
claims and plausible false mutations, because FEVER retrieval must retrieve
evidence for refutation as well as support.

For joint document-and-question generation, create Wikipedia-style passages and
mutated claims together. Useful synthetic data should include paraphrases,
negations, entity substitutions, date substitutions, relation substitutions, and
specificity changes. The positive document should contain evidence that directly
settles the claim. Avoid generating ordinary questions; the retrieval query
should remain a claim.
