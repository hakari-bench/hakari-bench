## Overview

SciFact is a scientific claim verification dataset where systems must retrieve
research abstracts that contain evidence for or against a scientific claim.
`NanoSciFact` is the compact English NanoBEIR retrieval version: each query is a
scientific claim, and relevant documents are scientific abstracts that support or
refute it in the original task. The benchmark tests evidence retrieval over
specialized biomedical and scientific language, including claims about disease,
screening, immunology, genetics, metabolism, and clinical outcomes.

## Details

### What the Original Data Measures

[Fact or Fiction: Verifying Scientific
Claims](https://arxiv.org/abs/2004.14974) introduces SciFact as a scientific
claim verification task: systems select abstracts from the research literature
that contain evidence supporting or refuting a claim, then identify rationale
sentences for the decision. The paper states that SciFact contains 1.4K
expert-written scientific claims paired with evidence-containing abstracts
annotated with labels and rationales.

The paper is important for interpreting this Nano task because SciFact claims
are not casual web claims. They are atomic scientific statements derived from
citation sentences, and verification requires domain knowledge, directionality,
statistics, quantities, and biological or clinical context. [BEIR: A
Heterogeneous Benchmark for Zero-shot Evaluation of Information Retrieval
Models](https://arxiv.org/abs/2104.08663) places SciFact in the Fact Checking
group with scientific claims and PubMed-style article documents. In the
NanoBEIR retrieval setting, the label distinction between support and refute is
not the evaluation target; the retriever must first find the evidence abstract.

### Observed Data Profile

The sampled Nano task has 50 queries, 2,919 documents, and 56 positive qrel rows.
Most queries have exactly one positive abstract; four queries have multiple
positives, with a maximum of four. Queries average 95.80 characters and are
usually precise scientific claims, for example `Cold exposure reduces BAT
recruitment`, `Macrolides protect against myocardial infarction`, and
`Insomnia can be effectively treated with cognitive behavioral therapy`.

Documents are biomedical or scientific abstracts with an average length of
1,431.23 characters. They often contain structured evidence: background,
methods, study population, measurements, results, and conclusions. Many positives
are close evidence matches, such as a claim about HPV screening retrieving an
abstract comparing HPV testing with cytology, or a claim about granuloma centers
retrieving an abstract on molecular maps of tuberculosis granulomas.

The task differs from FEVER-style Wikipedia fact checking because the evidence is
scientific literature. Models need to handle abbreviations, clinical endpoints,
causal direction, negation, measurements, and domain-specific entity names. A
retriever may not need to decide support versus refute, but it must retrieve the
abstract that contains enough evidence for that later reasoning step.

### BM25 Difficulty

Using the dataset-provided BM25 candidate column, BM25 reaches nDCG@10 = 0.7174
and hit@10 = 0.8600. BM25 ranks 29 of 56 positive qrel rows first, and 48
positives appear in the top 10. The median positive rank is 1. This high lexical
baseline is expected: many scientific claims contain distinctive terminology
that appears in the relevant abstract, such as `HPV`, `macrolides`, `granuloma`,
`Ly49Q`, or `PGE 2`.

The failures still matter. For `The deregulated and prolonged activation of
monocytes has deleterious effects in inflammatory diseases`, BM25 ranks general
monocyte and disease abstracts above the positive KLF2 atherosclerosis abstract,
which appears at rank 100. For the co-IR blockade cancer claim, BM25 finds
immunology and adverse-event documents but misses the broader immuno-oncology
evidence abstract until rank 100. For the glycolysis claim, lexical overlap finds
Warburg-effect and metabolic-inhibitor abstracts while the positive about
glycolysis powering fast axonal transport is rank 16. These cases require a
model to connect claim meaning to the evidence relation, not only shared terms.

Because support and refute labels are collapsed into binary retrieval relevance,
nDCG@10 should be read as evidence-location quality. A high-scoring retriever
gets the right abstracts into the candidate set for a downstream verifier; it
does not by itself establish that the model can judge the claim.

### Training Data That May Help

The official SciFact training split is the most directly relevant source for
scientific evidence retrieval and claim verification, provided evaluation
overlap is avoided. Useful auxiliary data includes non-overlapping scientific
claim/evidence pairs, PubMed abstract retrieval, biomedical entailment or NLI
data, citation-sentence-to-cited-abstract pairs, and rationale-labeled scientific
fact-checking datasets.

Training data should include both support and refute evidence, plus hard
negatives that share biomedical terminology but do not verify the claim. Upstream
development/test claims, evidence abstracts, and Nano evaluation qrels should not
be used as training examples.

### Synthetic Data Guidance

For document-to-question generation, use non-evaluation biomedical abstracts and
generate atomic scientific claims that are supported or refuted by the abstract.
Synthetic claims should vary in directionality, quantities, interventions,
populations, and outcomes, and should avoid vague statements that cannot be
verified from one source.

For joint generation, create realistic scientific abstracts with methods and
results, then generate several claims plus evidence labels and rationale spans.
Hard negatives should use similar entities or methods while changing the outcome,
population, or direction of effect. Do not seed generation with Nano evaluation
claims or positive abstracts.
