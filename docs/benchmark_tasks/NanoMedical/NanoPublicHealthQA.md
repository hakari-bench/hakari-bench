## Overview

`NanoPublicHealthQA` is an Arabic public-health FAQ retrieval task derived from
the `publichealth-qa` dataset. Queries are Arabic questions about COVID-19,
infection prevention, healthcare procedures, pregnancy, breastfeeding, malaria,
and influenza. Documents are Arabic answer passages translated or collected from
CDC and WHO FAQ/Q&A material. The task tests whether a retriever can match a
public-health question to the exact guidance-style answer passage.

## Details

### What the Original Data Measures

No standalone task paper or benchmark paper was confirmed for
`publichealth-qa`. The interpretation below is based on the official
[publichealth-qa](https://huggingface.co/datasets/xhluca/publichealth-qa)
Hugging Face dataset card, repository metadata, and the observed Nano sample
data. The dataset card says the question-answer pairs were sourced from CDC and
WHO Q&A pages and FAQs about COVID-19, produced and collected between December
2019 and April 2020, originally published as a Kaggle COVID-QA multilingual
split, and regenerated using the publichealth-qa repository. It also lists
Arabic, English, Spanish, and other languages, with source licensing from CDC and
WHO.

This task measures practical FAQ answer retrieval rather than open-domain
medical literature search. The query is a public-facing health question, and the
positive document is the corresponding guidance answer. The evidence is not a
scientific abstract or clinical trial paragraph; it is a public-health
communication passage that often contains instructions, cautions, and policy
language. The information need is therefore closer to "find the official FAQ
answer for this question" than to "find all evidence about this disease".

Because the observed split is Arabic, it also evaluates Arabic retrieval in a
highly specific health-information setting. A model must handle Arabic
question-answer wording, public-health terminology, WHO/CDC style, COVID-19
vocabulary, and procedural expressions such as isolation, personal protective
equipment, breastfeeding, disinfectants, and hospital triage. Domain precision is
important because many questions share the same disease name but ask different
actions.

### Observed Data Profile

The sampled task has 86 queries, 86 documents, and 86 positive qrel rows. Every
query has exactly one positive answer passage. Queries average 79.85 characters,
and documents average 828.15 characters, so the task is much more asymmetric
than duplicate-question retrieval: a short-to-medium question maps to a longer
answer paragraph. Several documents are several hundred to more than one
thousand characters and contain multiple recommendations in one passage.

The actual queries cluster around public-health decisions during the early
COVID-19 pandemic. They ask whether antibiotics work, whether parcels are safe,
how long the virus survives on surfaces, how to clean linens, whether suspected
patients need referral hospitals, whether pregnant or breastfeeding patients need
special handling, how COVID-19 compares with influenza, and how malaria-endemic
countries should respond. Many questions contain the same high-frequency disease
terms: `كوفيد-19`, `فيروس كورونا`, `العدوى`, `المريض`, and `المنظمة`. The
relevant answer depends on the action or relation in the question, not only on
the disease name.

The documents have official-guidance style. They often begin with a direct
answer such as "لا" or "نعم", followed by explanation, conditions, and
recommended actions. Some answers include enumerations or multiple clauses:
which personal protective equipment to wear, how to clean contaminated laundry,
which disinfectants to use, or how to continue breastfeeding safely. This style
creates a retrieval problem where a correct passage may be lexically dense and
long, while the deciding cue may be a phrase about a particular action.

### BM25 Difficulty

Using the dataset-provided BM25 candidate column, BM25 reaches nDCG@10 = 0.6722
and hit@10 = 0.8372. BM25 ranks 43 of 86 positives first and 72 of 86 positives
inside the top 10. This is a reasonably strong sparse baseline because many
queries and answers share distinctive Arabic health terms. When the question
contains narrow terms such as breastfeeding, antibiotics, disinfectants, malaria,
or influenza, lexical overlap can directly point to the answer passage.

The failures reveal the main difficulty: COVID-19 terms are shared by almost the
entire corpus, so lexical matching often retrieves topically adjacent but
actionally wrong guidance. For the question asking whether pregnant patients with
confirmed or suspected COVID-19 should have a cesarean section, BM25 ranks
general pregnancy-care passages above the precise answer that cesarean delivery
should be limited to medically justified cases. For the question about animal
source transmission, BM25 ranks passages about first infections, ineffective
measures, or blood transmission above the specific zoonotic-source explanation.
For the question about severe-risk groups, it retrieves general masking,
incubation, or smoking-risk passages before the answer about older adults and
people with pre-existing conditions.

This means the task rewards fine-grained public-health intent matching. A strong
model should distinguish "what is COVID-19", "how is it transmitted", "what
should pregnant patients do", "what should hospitals do", and "what actions are
not effective", even though all share the same pandemic vocabulary.

### Training Data That May Help

Useful existing data includes non-overlapping public-health FAQ pairs in Arabic
and other languages, WHO/CDC-style question-answer data, medical FAQ retrieval
pairs, and Arabic healthcare guidance documents paired with user questions.
Because the source dataset is small and public, data likely to overlap with this
benchmark, such as the same publichealth-qa examples or regenerated copies of
the same Kaggle COVID-QA multilingual split, should preferably be excluded from
training when evaluating on this task.

General biomedical abstract retrieval is not the best match by itself. This task
is about official public guidance, so useful training should teach procedural
answer retrieval, risk communication, public-facing wording, and short question
to longer answer matching. Arabic native-language supervision is especially
valuable; translating English FAQ pairs may help, but it should not replace
native Arabic phrasing and terminology.

### Synthetic Data Guidance

For document-to-question generation, start from non-evaluation Arabic public
health guidance passages and generate Arabic questions that ask about one
specific recommendation, exception, comparison, or safety condition. Good
questions should include user-facing formulations such as "هل يمكن", "كيف",
"ما هي", and "هل من الآمن", and the generated answer passage should contain the
exact recommendation needed to answer the question.

For joint document-and-question generation, create realistic Arabic FAQ answer
passages about public-health scenarios: vaccination, respiratory infection,
sanitation, pregnancy and breastfeeding, school or workplace exposure, travel,
high-risk groups, and treatment availability. Then create short questions that
target one actionable detail in the passage. The synthetic documents should use
official guidance style with conditions, cautions, and concrete actions rather
than informal medical chat. Do not seed generation with Nano evaluation queries
or positive passages.
