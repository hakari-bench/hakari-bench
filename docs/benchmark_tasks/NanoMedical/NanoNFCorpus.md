## Overview

`NanoNFCorpus` is an English medical and nutrition retrieval task derived from
NFCorpus. Queries are short layperson-facing health and nutrition topics, many
of them only a few words long. Documents are technical medical article titles and
abstracts, mostly from PubMed and PubMed Central. The task tests whether a
retriever can bridge the lexical gap between consumer health language and
scientific biomedical evidence.

## Details

### What the Original Data Measures

[A Full-Text Learning to Rank Dataset for Medical Information Retrieval](https://www.cl.uni-heidelberg.de/~sokolov/pubs/boteva16full.pdf)
introduces NFCorpus as a learning-to-rank dataset for medical information
retrieval. The paper says its queries come from NutritionFacts.org, a
non-commercial site that presents nutrition and health topics in lay English,
while the documents are research articles linked from the site, mainly hosted on
PubMed. The paper emphasizes the medical retrieval lexical gap: users may search
with everyday health language, while the relevant documents use technical
biomedical terminology.

The official [NFCorpus page](https://www.cl.uni-heidelberg.de/statnlpgroup/nfcorpus/)
describes 3,244 natural-language queries, 169,756 automatically extracted
relevance judgments, and 9,964 medical documents. It also explains the three
levels of relevance: direct links from a NutritionFacts page to a cited medical
article, indirect links through another internally linked NutritionFacts page,
and weaker topic/tag-based links. The data is split by query into training,
development, and test subsets.

This task is not ordinary FAQ answer retrieval. It asks models to retrieve
scientific abstracts that support or relate to a health-topic page. A short query
such as `BMAA`, `Hiroshima`, `growth promoters`, or `caloric restriction` may
map to long biomedical abstracts where the key evidence is expressed with
specific study terminology.

### Observed Data Profile

The Nano split has 200 queries, 3,593 documents, and 3,718 positive qrel rows.
It is heavily multi-positive: the average query has 18.59 positives, the median
is 9, and 160 of 200 queries have more than one positive. Queries are very short,
averaging 17.15 characters; the shortest observed queries include `BPH`, `DHA`,
`Alli`, `BMAA`, `coma`, `ECMO`, `lard`, `milk`, `nuts`, and `okra`. Documents
average 1,589.52 characters and usually contain an article title followed by an
abstract.

The sampled queries include nutrition topics, food items, public-health
references, and biomedical terms. Some are ordinary health questions, such as
whether caffeinated tea is dehydrating or how cooked meat carcinogens can be
avoided. Others are terse topic labels from NutritionFacts-style pages. This
creates a broad retrieval surface: a model must handle exact biomedical acronyms,
food names, geography or exposure labels, and consumer-health questions.

### BM25 Difficulty

Using the dataset-provided BM25 candidate column, BM25 reaches nDCG@10 = 0.2434
and hit@10 = 0.5650. BM25 finds at least one positive in the top 10 for just over
half of the queries, but it retrieves only 320 of 3,718 positive qrel rows inside
the top 10. The median rank of individual positives is 80 among the provided BM25
candidates.

The failures show the expected NFCorpus difficulty. For `Do Cholesterol Statin
Drugs Cause Breast Cancer?`, BM25 retrieves statin and breast-cancer abstracts
but misses a positive about statins offsetting cardiovascular risk from dietary
fat. For `Chronic Headaches and Pork Parasites`, it retrieves pork cysticercus
and headache-management abstracts before the neurocysticercosis review. For
`BMAA`, it ranks other BMAA neurotoxicity papers above a positive shark-fin
exposure paper. Sparse retrieval often reaches the right vocabulary cluster, but
the correct relevance relation may depend on the NutritionFacts article's
argument, citation role, or topic link rather than simple keyword overlap.

### Training Data That May Help

Useful training data includes non-overlapping consumer-health-to-biomedical
retrieval pairs, nutrition article citation links, biomedical abstract retrieval
with lay queries, and hard negatives from the same disease, food, or exposure
topic. Training should teach both exact acronym matching and semantic bridging
between consumer phrasing and technical abstracts.

Because NFCorpus has official train/dev/test splits, clean evaluation should
exclude overlapping NFCorpus test queries, qrels, and source NutritionFacts page
links. Models trained on the official NFCorpus train split may still be useful
for in-domain learning, but results should distinguish that setting from
zero-shot evaluation.

### Synthetic Data Guidance

For document-to-question generation, start from non-evaluation biomedical
abstracts and generate short layperson health-topic queries: food names,
exposure concerns, disease-prevention claims, supplement questions, or concise
NutritionFacts-style titles. The generated query should not simply copy the
article title; it should express how a general reader might refer to the health
topic.

For joint document-and-question generation, create consumer-health article
titles or questions and pair them with technical PubMed-like abstracts. Include
hard negatives from the same food, nutrient, disease, or mechanism but with a
different outcome or study population. Do not seed synthetic examples with Nano
evaluation queries or positive abstracts.
