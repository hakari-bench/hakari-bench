## Overview

`NanoCmedqa` is a Chinese medical consultation retrieval task based on CMedQA
and represented in NanoMedical as a compact retrieval split. Queries are
patient-style Chinese medical questions, and documents are candidate consultation
answers. Compared with the CMedQAv2 reranking split, this task is closer to the
out-of-domain cMedQA retrieval setting discussed in DuReader-retrieval: a model
must retrieve useful answers from a larger candidate pool, with short,
informal, and sometimes noisy medical text.

## Details

### What the Original Data Measures

[DuReader-Retrieval: A Large-scale Chinese Benchmark for Passage Retrieval from Web Search Engine](https://arxiv.org/abs/2203.10232)
uses cMedQA as one of two out-of-domain test sets for evaluating cross-domain
Chinese passage retrieval. The paper states that cMedQA focuses on medical
question-answering conversations and randomly selects 3,999 samples for the
out-of-domain test data. In its cMedQA out-of-domain results, BM25 obtains
MRR@10 = 6.26, Recall@1 = 4.98, and Recall@50 = 14.05, while a dense retriever
trained on in-domain DuReader-retrieval data does not transfer well until
fine-tuned on the target domain.

The underlying medical QA source is CMedQAv2. The official
[cMedQA2 repository](https://github.com/zhangsheng93/cMedQA2) describes the data
as Chinese community medical question answering, anonymized for privacy and
available for non-commercial research. The repository reports 108,000 questions
and 203,569 answers, with average lengths around 49 characters per question and
101 characters per answer. The CMedQAv2 paper,
[Multi-Scale Attentive Interaction Networks for Chinese Medical Question Answer Selection](https://doi.org/10.1109/ACCESS.2018.2883637),
frames the task as Chinese medical question answer selection.

This task therefore measures retrieval under a domain-transfer medical QA
setting: matching messy patient questions to useful Chinese answer passages,
where lexical overlap is common but often not sufficient.

### Observed Data Profile

The Nano split has 200 queries, 10,000 documents, and 324 positive qrel rows.
The average query has 1.62 positives; 70 of 200 queries have more than one
positive, and the maximum is 9. Queries average 52.00 characters, and documents
average 157.57 characters, which is longer than the CMedQAv2 reranking split in
NanoMedical.

The observed questions contain colloquial patient histories: delayed
menstruation, pregnancy checks, pediatric fever, gynecological symptoms,
fractures, semen analysis, hysteria-like emotional episodes, balanitis, and
post-surgical pregnancy concerns. Documents are usually medical-advice answers,
but the sampled corpus also shows noise and loosely related text. Some top BM25
negatives are generic, partial, or even non-medical-looking passages, so robust
models need to handle both domain vocabulary and corpus cleanliness.

### BM25 Difficulty

Using the dataset-provided BM25 candidate column, BM25 reaches nDCG@10 = 0.1668
and hit@10 = 0.2750. BM25 ranks 27 positives first and 69 of 324 positives
inside the top 10. This is slightly higher than the CMedQAv2 reranking Nano
split on nDCG@10, but still a difficult sparse baseline.

The failure cases are clinically close but answer-specific. For a pregnancy
checkup question, BM25 retrieves another pregnancy-examination answer first, but
misses the positive that lays out NT, Down-syndrome screening, four-dimensional
ultrasound, and late-pregnancy check intervals. For a semen-analysis question,
BM25 retrieves a general normal-semen description before the positive answer
with detailed reference values and abnormality interpretation. For a fractured
ankle question, a noisy non-medical passage ranks first, showing that term
overlap can be brittle in this corpus. The task rewards answer grounding and
medical-intent matching more than raw keyword matching.

### Training Data That May Help

Useful training data includes non-overlapping Chinese medical QA retrieval,
Chinese medical answer-selection data, doctor-patient consultation corpora,
symptom-to-advice retrieval pairs, and hard negatives drawn from the same
medical specialty. Data that teaches a model to normalize colloquial symptoms,
pregnancy timing, examination names, and medication advice should transfer well.

To avoid leakage, training should exclude CMedQA/CMedQAv2 rows overlapping the
Nano split and should deduplicate against community medical QA corpora scraped
from the same source sites. Because DuReader-retrieval used cMedQA as an
out-of-domain test set, models trained directly on that cMedQA test data would
not be clean zero-shot retrieval systems for this task.

### Synthetic Data Guidance

For document-to-question generation, start from non-evaluation Chinese medical
advice answers and generate realistic patient questions with symptoms, duration,
age, sex or pregnancy context, examination results, and a concrete concern. The
generated question should be concise but may include repetition and informal
wording.

For joint document-and-question generation, create one patient question and
several candidate answers. Correct answers should address the specific concern,
while hard negatives should share disease names or symptoms but differ in test
interpretation, patient context, timing, or recommended treatment. Include some
short generic-looking negatives so the model learns to reject superficially
similar but unhelpful replies. Do not seed generation with Nano evaluation text.
