## Overview

BIRCO's DORIS-MAE task models scientific literature search by computer
scientists with complex research needs. A query is a first-person,
paragraph-length request that names desired methods, evaluation settings,
applications, or constraints, and the retriever must select abstracts that meet
those requirements. In this Nano split, most queries have one positive but some
broad research needs have many, so the task mixes exact abstract matching with
coverage of a researcher's stated intent.

## Details

### What the Original Data Measures

[BIRCO: A Benchmark of Information Retrieval Tasks with Complex
Objectives](https://arxiv.org/abs/2402.14151) includes DORIS-MAE as one of five
complex-objective retrieval tasks. The paper describes DORIS-MAE as 60 complex
research questions from computer scientists, where each query communicates
specific requirements from research papers and candidate pools have around 110
documents. The task objective prompt in the appendix says the goal is to
identify the abstract that most effectively meets the user's needs.

The BIRCO paper emphasizes that DORIS-MAE queries are multi-faceted. It reports
an average of 5.6 facets per query, where facets are semantic components in the
research need. DORIS-MAE also uses a multi-level relevance signal in the source
task, which means many abstracts can be partially or strongly relevant to the
same complex request.

BIRCO's broader construction matters here. The benchmark was designed for long
queries, low lexical overlap, small candidate pools, and reduced contamination
for LLM-based retrieval evaluation. For DORIS-MAE, the paper reports that GPT-4
was unable to answer the queries successfully without candidate documents during
decontamination checks, so none were removed for that reason.

### Observed Data Profile

The sampled Nano task has 60 queries, 5,544 documents, and 1,569 positive qrel
rows. The average query has 26.15 positive abstracts, but the distribution is
skewed: 44 queries have a single positive row, while some broad research needs
have up to 100 positives. Queries average 995.53 characters and are written as
first-person research needs with constraints, desired methods, evaluation
settings, and intended applications.

Documents average 1,220.27 characters and are scientific abstracts, mostly from
computer science and adjacent fields. The observed positives cover statistical
linguistics, controllable text generation, human-like causal reasoning,
expressive graph neural networks, interactive learning with noisy feedback,
pedestrian tracking, quantitative logic reasoning, historical linguistics,
German emotion-stimulus extraction, and image/document compression topics.

The task is difficult because relevance is holistic. A query may mention several
requirements, such as scalability, model architecture, domain, data modality,
task type, and desired evaluation. A relevant abstract may satisfy only some
facets, and the Nano qrels include many positives for some queries. The model
must rank abstracts by how well they satisfy the whole research need.

### BM25 Difficulty

Using the dataset-provided BM25 candidate column, BM25 reaches nDCG@10 = 0.2469
and hit@10 = 0.4167 when relevance is treated as membership in the Nano qrels.
BM25 retrieves at least one positive abstract in the top 10 for 25 of 60
queries, and only 113 of 1,569 positive qrel rows appear in the top 10.

The inspected failures show that lexical anchors are often misleading. For a
customer-feedback topic segmentation query, BM25 retrieves fashion recommendation
and relevance-feedback papers before a general interactive-learning framework.
For an autonomous-driving pedestrian-risk query, papers about pedestrian
interaction with vehicles outrank a multi-pedestrian aerial-tracking abstract.
For a quantitative-reasoning query, social-network logical reasoning and XAI
papers outrank a paper about sciduction and quantitative verification. For an
HDR-to-JPEG-XT compression query, BM25 ranks the most literal JPEG XT papers
above the sampled positive about identity document recognition, illustrating
that qrels can encode broader or unexpected relevance judgments.

Because DORIS-MAE is multi-positive and multi-faceted, hit@10 is only a coarse
signal. nDCG@10 better reflects whether the retrieved abstracts collectively
cover the user's technical requirements.

### Training Data That May Help

Useful training data should include non-overlapping complex scientific search
pairs where long research needs are matched to abstracts. DORIS-MAE development
or training data should be inspected before use and excluded if it overlaps with
the Nano evaluation split. Auxiliary data could include paper recommendation
datasets, citation intent retrieval, expert-written research questions paired
with abstracts, and hard negatives from the same research area that satisfy only
some facets.

Training should preserve multi-facet relevance. Good negatives are not random
abstracts; they are abstracts that match the topic but miss the method,
modality, scalability constraint, evaluation setting, or application.

### Synthetic Data Guidance

For document-to-question generation, start from non-evaluation abstracts and
generate paragraph-length research needs that include method, task, data type,
constraints, and evaluation desiderata. For joint generation, create a research
need plus several abstracts that vary in facet coverage.

Do not seed generation with Nano evaluation queries or positive passages.
Synthetic data should include partial matches and same-topic hard negatives so
the model learns to rank abstracts by holistic fit rather than by isolated
keywords.
