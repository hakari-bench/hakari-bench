## Overview

BIRCO's WhatsThatBook task captures an informal but complex retrieval goal:
users describe a half-remembered book using plot fragments, cover details,
approximate dates, character relationships, and uncertain genre cues. The Nano
query is such a memory post, and the target is the book description that
holistically best fits it. The task is difficult because relevant evidence is
distributed across many weak clues, so lexical overlap with one remembered
detail is not enough.

## Details

### What the Original Data Measures

[BIRCO: A Benchmark of Information Retrieval Tasks with Complex
Objectives](https://arxiv.org/abs/2402.14151) describes WhatsThatBook as a
literature retrieval task where users provide ambiguous descriptions of books.
The paper's task objective says the passage should be the book description that
holistically best matches the explicit or implicit details in the query. This is
not simple semantic similarity: a query can contain remembered plot fragments,
cover details, approximate dates, character relationships, and uncertainty.

The BIRCO paper reports that WhatsThatBook has the lowest lexical overlap among
its tasks and the highest average number of query facets, about 11.3 details per
query. It also reports aggressive decontamination: 56% of WhatsThatBook queries
were removed because GPT-4 could potentially guess the book without candidate
documents. The retained task is therefore focused on cases where comparing the
candidate descriptions is necessary.

### Observed Data Profile

The sampled Nano task has 100 queries, 1,766 documents, and 100 positive qrel
rows. Every query has exactly one positive book description. Queries average
811.34 characters and are informal memory posts. Documents average 1,091.24
characters and are book descriptions, often with repeated synopsis text.

The observed positives cover twin girls with magical powers, a bullied
schoolgirl's birthday party memory, a traffic-jam/radio-show novel, a children's
zoo pun book, a magical island reached through a periodic door, romance and
thriller plots, fantasy portal stories, dystopian forensic investigation, New
England family magic, and disability after a train crash. The queries often
state approximate read dates, cover memories, uncertainty, and details that may
be wrong.

The task rewards robust matching across noisy memories. The model must combine
many weak clues rather than rely on one term. A candidate may share a genre,
setting, or character type but miss the decisive combination of details.

### BM25 Difficulty

Using the dataset-provided BM25 candidate column, BM25 reaches nDCG@10 = 0.0751
and hit@10 = 0.1100 on this Nano split. BM25 places 4 of 100 positives at rank
1 and only 11 of 100 positives in the top 10. This shows that lexical retrieval
struggles badly with ambiguous book memories.

The inspected failures show familiar tip-of-the-tongue problems. For a mountain
cabin abduction/romance memory, BM25 retrieves other romance or suspense books
with overlapping relationship terms before the correct mountain-cabin
description. For a fantasy novel where two girls enter the `Queen of Twilight`
book, unrelated fantasy and romance titles outrank the correct portal-fantasy
description. For a dystopian forensic investigator query with victims named
Emily, pages mentioning Emily or crime-like plots outrank the exact Luke Harding
book. For the magical island door query, other missing-child or magical-danger
descriptions outrank the correct book even though the user's detailed clues are
distinctive.

Because this split is single-positive, hit@10 is a direct measure of whether
the remembered book appears in the candidate shortlist. nDCG@10 is very low
because even when BM25 sees overlapping clues, it rarely ranks the full-detail
match near the top.

### Training Data That May Help

Useful training data should include non-overlapping book-finding posts matched
to book descriptions, tip-of-the-tongue retrieval data, and synthetic memory
queries paired with descriptions. WhatsThatBook or BIRCO development data
should be checked for overlap before use. Good hard negatives share genre,
setting, era, or one memorable clue while missing the full combination.

Training should model uncertainty and partial recall. The query may say "I
think", "maybe", "not sure", or give approximate dates. The model needs to
weigh many soft clues instead of requiring exact wording.

### Synthetic Data Guidance

For document-to-question generation, start from non-evaluation book
descriptions and generate noisy user memory posts with approximate read dates,
cover recollections, character details, plot fragments, and uncertain language.
For joint generation, create book descriptions and several plausible but wrong
memory posts or distractor descriptions.

Do not seed generation with Nano evaluation queries or positive passages.
Synthetic negatives should share genre or one striking clue but differ in the
book's core combination of plot, characters, setting, and ending.
