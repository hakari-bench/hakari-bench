## Overview

NanoDAPFAM is the Nano task group for DAPFAM, a domain-aware patent-family
retrieval benchmark. It evaluates citation-linked prior-art retrieval at the
patent-family level across eighteen variants. The variants combine three domain
conditions (`ALL`, `IN`, and `OUT`), two query representations (title-abstract
or title-abstract-claims), and three target representations (title-abstract,
title-abstract-claims, or full text).

The group is useful because it separates ordinary lexical patent similarity from
cross-domain prior-art retrieval. Same-domain and all-domain variants often give
BM25 many technical anchors. OUT-domain variants remove shared IPC3 technical
classes, so the model must find cited families whose relation may be a
transferable mechanism, component, material, or method rather than the same
surface vocabulary.

## Details

### What the Original Group Measures

[DAPFAM: A Domain-Aware Family-level Dataset to benchmark cross domain patent retrieval](https://arxiv.org/abs/2506.22141)
introduces a family-level patent retrieval benchmark with citation-based
relevance judgments and explicit domain partitions. The source paper describes a
dataset with 1,247 query families and 45,336 target families, aggregated at the
family level to reduce international patent redundancy. It defines IN-domain and
OUT-domain relations using IPC3 overlap, so retrieval systems can be evaluated
on both same-domain prior-art search and harder cross-domain discovery.

NanoDAPFAM keeps the DAPFAM structure but samples compact 200-query splits. The
same underlying family-level citation relations are evaluated under different
text representations. Title-abstract queries are compact patent summaries.
Title-abstract-claims queries add long claim language. Target documents can be
short title-abstract records, claim-rich title-abstract-claims records, or long
full-text records that include descriptions. This makes the group a controlled
probe of how domain partition and patent-field length change retrieval behavior.

### Subtask Coverage

The eighteen subtasks form a complete matrix:

- **Domain condition:** `All` includes both same-IPC3 and cross-IPC3 positives,
  `In` keeps same-domain positives, and `Out` keeps cross-domain positives.
- **Query representation:** `TitlAbs` uses title plus abstract; `TitlAbsClm`
  adds claims to the query.
- **Target representation:** `ToTitlAbs` targets title plus abstract,
  `ToTitlAbsClm` adds claims, and `ToFullText` targets long full patent-family
  text.

All observed NanoDAPFAM splits are English patent retrieval tasks. Every split
is multi-positive. ALL variants usually have almost 20 positives per query,
IN-domain variants have a wider but still high qrel count distribution, and
OUT-domain variants include many single-positive or low-positive queries.

### Observed Group Profile

Across the eighteen splits, NanoDAPFAM contains 3,600 queries, 49,879 positive
qrels, and 180,000 split-local candidate documents. Each split has 200 queries
and 10,000 candidate documents. The document count is a sum across subtasks, not
a deduplicated group-wide corpus size.

Queries average 4,732.44 characters when weighted by query count. This mean is
driven by the claim-bearing query variants, where title-abstract-claims queries
average roughly 8,300 to 9,300 characters. Title-abstract-only queries average
roughly 770 to 790 characters. Target length depends even more on representation:
title-abstract targets average about 778 characters, title-abstract-claims
targets average about 7,200 characters, and full-text targets average roughly
69,000 to 72,000 characters.

The sampled records are patent-family texts rather than user search queries.
They contain titles, abstracts, claims, descriptions, IPC-related technical
vocabulary, apparatus descriptions, materials, processes, problem-solution
phrasing, and legal claim wording. Observed query-positive pairs include
livestock measurement to contactless mass estimation, soil remediation to tar
sands treatment, extreme ultraviolet light sources to lithium sputtering, and
vehicle lighting to anti-reflective glass processing.

### BM25 Difficulty

Query-weighted BM25 nDCG@10 is 0.4622 and query-weighted hit@10 is 0.6142, but
the average hides the domain split. ALL and IN-domain variants are strong for a
lexical baseline: their hit@10 values range from 0.7550 to 0.8600. OUT-domain
variants are much harder: hit@10 ranges from 0.1150 to 0.2350, and the hardest
split, `NanoDAPFAMOutTitlAbsClmToTitlAbs`, has nDCG@10 = 0.0719.

The source paper reports a pronounced cross-domain gap, and the Nano data shows
the same pattern. When query and target families share IPC3 areas, BM25 can use
technical keywords, components, and claim phrases. When positives are
OUT-domain, the relation may depend on an analogy or transferred mechanism, and
short target abstracts often lack the lexical evidence needed by BM25. Adding
claims or full text to the target helps somewhat because it exposes more
mechanism-level terms, but it does not remove the cross-domain gap.

The representation matrix also matters. Title-abstract targets are compact and
less noisy, while full-text targets expose more vocabulary but introduce very
long patent descriptions. Claim-bearing targets often improve OUT-domain
lexical retrieval because claims include components and operations that may not
appear in the target abstract.

### Training Data That May Help

Useful training data includes patent-family citation retrieval, prior-art search
pairs, patent semantic similarity, cross-IPC citation prediction, patent analogy
retrieval, and hard negatives drawn from the same IPC3 area as the query. Models
should see title, abstract, claim, and description fields separately as well as
combined representations, because the Nano group explicitly tests
representation changes.

Training should exclude NanoDAPFAM evaluation family IDs, qrels, positive target
families, same-family duplicate publications, and source rows used in the Nano
splits. Family-level aggregation matters: treating individual patent
publications as independent examples can leak the same invention through another
jurisdiction or family member.

### Synthetic Data Guidance

Synthetic patent retrieval data should preserve the family-level and
domain-aware structure. Generate patent titles, abstracts, claims, and
description-like full text, then create citation-style positives that share a
technical dependency, improvement relation, component reuse, material, process,
or problem-solution pattern. For OUT-domain simulation, positives should cross
technical classes while retaining a plausible transferable mechanism.

Hard negatives should be close but wrong. Same-domain negatives can share IPC3
codes and terminology without being cited or technically dependent. OUT-domain
negatives can share surface words while solving a different problem. Synthetic
full-text examples should keep long legal and technical structure without simply
padding the target with repeated query terms.
