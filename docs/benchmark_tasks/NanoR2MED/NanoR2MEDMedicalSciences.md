## Overview

`NanoR2MEDMedicalSciences` is an English R2MED Q&A reference retrieval task.
Queries are Medical Sciences StackExchange posts about health, physiology,
nutrition, symptoms, and medical practice. Documents are external webpages or
medical-reference passages that support the accepted answer. The task measures
whether a retriever can connect consumer-facing or practitioner-facing medical
questions to the specific explanatory evidence needed to answer them.

## Details

### What the Original Data Measures

[R2MED: A Benchmark for Reasoning-Driven Medical Retrieval](https://arxiv.org/abs/2505.14558)
positions R2MED as a reasoning-driven alternative to medical retrieval datasets
where direct lexical or semantic similarity is often enough. The paper states
that the Q&A reference retrieval task uses StackExchange posts as queries and
answer-linked webpages as evidence documents, with the answer acting as an
implicit reasoning anchor.

For Medical Sciences, R2MED selects StackExchange posts with accepted or highly
upvoted answers and at least one external URL. The paper reports that
`medicine_wiki` is used as a domain-relevant negative corpus for this dataset,
and that documents are sentence-split and regrouped into approximately
128-token passages. Candidate positives are expanded by a retrieval committee
and then filtered by GPT-4o relevance assessment and expert review.

### Observed Data Profile

The Nano split has 88 queries, 10,000 documents, and 244 positive qrels. Queries
average 477.62 characters, and documents average 678.60 characters. The sampled
queries cover microwave food preparation, acne during weight loss, swallowing
pills, water intake frequency, and protein supplements.

Each query has 2.77 positives on average, with a median of 2 and a maximum of
8 positives. The data mixes everyday health concerns with technical medical
concepts. Some posts include off-topic closure text or community framing, so
retrievers must focus on the underlying medical intent rather than all visible
forum boilerplate.

### BM25 Difficulty

Using the dataset-provided BM25 candidate column, BM25 reaches nDCG@10 = 0.1043
and hit@10 = 0.2727. It ranks 5 positives first and finds a positive inside the
top 10 for 24 of 88 queries. Sparse matching is weaker here than in Biology
because health questions often use broad everyday wording while positives may
be physiology, nutrition, or public-health reference passages.

Observed failures include a seasonal-coronavirus question where BM25 retrieves
COVID recall and surface-contamination text before a coronavirus chapter, a
corticosteroid/COVID question where it retrieves allergy text before cortisol
immunology, and a computer-health question where it retrieves general computer
content before sedentary-time and ergonomics evidence.

### Training Data That May Help

Useful training data includes non-overlapping Medical Sciences StackExchange
answer-link retrieval, consumer health QA with cited evidence, medical
encyclopedia section retrieval, NutritionFacts- or PubMed-style evidence
retrieval, and hard negatives from adjacent health topics. Because most queries
have multiple positives, multi-positive contrastive or listwise training should
be preferred over a single-positive simplification.

Training should avoid R2MED Medical Sciences evaluation posts, qrels, and
positive passages. Consumer health forum data should be de-duplicated against
the evaluated questions and answer-linked documents.

### Synthetic Data Guidance

For document-to-question generation, use non-evaluation health reference pages,
nutrition/physiology explanations, or evidence summaries and generate
StackExchange-style questions that include realistic user uncertainty,
misconceptions, or context.

For joint generation, create source-style medical reference snippets and paired
questions about symptoms, prevention, nutrition, medication effects, or
physiology. Hard negatives should share common health words but differ in the
mechanism, population, or recommendation. Do not use Nano evaluation examples as
synthetic seeds.
