## Overview

`ru_bq` is the RuBQRetrieval task from ruMTEB. Queries are Russian open-domain
questions derived from the RuBQ 2.0 knowledge-base QA dataset, and documents are
Russian Wikipedia paragraphs. The retriever must find paragraphs that provide
evidence for the answer, often when the paragraph names the entity or answer but
does not repeat the exact phrasing of the question.

## Details

### What the Original Data Measures

[The Russian-focused embedders' exploration: ruMTEB benchmark and Russian
embedding model design](https://arxiv.org/abs/2408.12503) includes
RuBQRetrieval as a ruMTEB retrieval task and states that it uses unique
paragraphs from RuBQ 2.0, with documents deduplicated for retrieval. The task
evaluates retrieval with nDCG@10 alongside the other ruMTEB retrieval datasets.

[RuBQ 2.0: An Innovated Russian Question Answering Dataset](https://openreview.net/forum?id=P5UQFFoQ4PJ)
introduces RuBQ 2.0 as a Russian KBQA dataset over Wikidata, with 2,910
questions, answers, SPARQL queries, and answer-bearing Wikipedia paragraphs for
many questions. The paper reports that questions came from query suggestion
services and RuBQ 1.0, then underwent crowdsourced and in-house annotation. It
also notes that relevant paragraphs are manually verified and that questions are
generated independently from paragraphs, making paragraph retrieval more natural
and less lexically tied than SQuAD-style question generation from a paragraph.

### Observed Data Profile

The Nano split has 200 queries, 10,000 documents, and 334 positive qrel rows.
Queries average 52.19 characters and are concise Russian fact questions, often
asking for a capital, river, author, mother, alternate name, institution, or
location. Documents average 484.49 characters and are Wikipedia paragraphs, with
some very short paragraphs and a few long article excerpts.

The qrels are moderately multi-positive: 89 of 200 queries have more than one
positive, with up to four positives. Many positives are not neatly answer-only
snippets; they are paragraphs from broader articles where the needed answer is
embedded among other facts. This makes the task a good test of evidence
retrieval for KBQA rather than exact answer-string lookup.

### BM25 Difficulty

Using the dataset-provided BM25 candidate column, BM25 reaches nDCG@10 = 0.5132
and hit@10 = 0.7000. BM25 often succeeds when the answer entity or the subject
entity appears in both query and paragraph, but it struggles when the query asks
for a relation that is expressed indirectly in the passage.

Observed hard cases include questions such as where the House of Bernadotte
rules, what city "The Prince and the Pauper" takes place in, and what the
capital of Cambodia is. BM25 frequently retrieves paragraphs that share words
like "столица", "город", or a familiar entity name but do not contain the
specific answer evidence. Some sampled positives are ranked around 100 even when
they clearly answer the question, showing that the task rewards relation-aware
matching and not just lexical overlap.

### Training Data That May Help

Useful training data includes non-overlapping RuBQ development questions and
supporting paragraphs if benchmark rules allow them, Russian KBQA
question-paragraph pairs, Wikidata-to-Wikipedia evidence retrieval data, and
Russian open-domain QA data with paragraph-level evidence. Training should
exclude RuBQRetrieval test queries, qrels, and answer-bearing paragraphs used by
the Nano split.

Because many queries can have multiple supporting paragraphs, training should
preserve multi-positive labels or use listwise objectives. Entity-linking and
relation-focused negatives are especially useful: the model should learn that a
paragraph about the same person, book, state, or dynasty is not enough unless it
contains the requested relation.

### Synthetic Data Guidance

For document-to-question generation, use non-evaluation Russian Wikipedia
paragraphs and generate concise Russian KBQA-style questions whose answer is
explicitly supported by the paragraph. Include relation types common in RuBQ:
capital, location, author, family relation, dynasty, work setting, alternate
name, and organization.

For joint generation, create Wikipedia-like paragraphs with clear entity facts
and then generate natural Russian questions from those facts. Include hard
negatives from the same article or same entity class that omit the answer
relation. Do not use Nano evaluation questions or positive paragraphs as
synthetic seeds.
