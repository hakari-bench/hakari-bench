## Overview

`ria_news` is a Russian news retrieval task from ruMTEB. Queries are RIA
headline strings and documents are the corresponding or distractor article
bodies from the Rossiya Segodnya news corpus. The retriever must match a compact
headline to the article it describes, usually with strong lexical overlap but
also with many near-duplicate political, sports, disaster, and market-news
documents.

## Details

### What the Original Data Measures

[The Russian-focused embedders' exploration: ruMTEB benchmark and Russian
embedding model design](https://arxiv.org/abs/2408.12503) defines
RiaNewsRetrieval as a ruMTEB retrieval task and reports that the final retrieval
dataset uses 10,000 randomly sampled headlines as queries and 724,344 article
texts as documents. The paper states that source articles were lowercased,
cleaned from HTML, normalized, filtered for length, and deduplicated by headline
and text.

The underlying RIA corpus was introduced in
[Self-Attentive Model for Headline Generation](https://arxiv.org/abs/1901.07786),
which describes a Rossiya Segodnya dataset of 1,003,869 Russian news articles
from January 2010 to December 2014, with average title length of 9.5 words and
average text length of 315.6 words. That paper introduced the corpus for
headline generation; ruMTEB repurposes it as headline-to-article retrieval.

### Observed Data Profile

The Nano split has 200 queries, 10,000 documents, and 200 positive qrel rows.
Every query has exactly one positive. Queries average 61.99 characters and are
lowercase news headlines. Documents average 1,145.34 characters and are
lowercase article bodies, often with agency style, quotes, dates, named
officials, and event follow-up details.

Most sampled examples are straightforward headline-to-article matches, such as a
market-index headline retrieving an article whose first sentence repeats the
index movements. The data also contains quirks visible in the Nano sample: a few
very short non-Russian-looking queries or documents appear, and some hard cases
have many topically similar news stories that share locations, casualties, or
political entities but describe a different event.

### BM25 Difficulty

Using the dataset-provided BM25 candidate column, BM25 reaches nDCG@10 = 0.8479
and hit@10 = 0.9150. This is the strongest BM25 behavior among the NanoRuMTEB
splits inspected here because many headlines share names, places, and event
phrases with their article body.

The hard cases show why the task is not only exact matching. BM25 misses some
short or noisy queries such as `liza` and `edvard`, and can retrieve a different
article with overlapping disaster or geography terms, such as another landslide
story for an Italy landslide headline. For entity-heavy political and sports
headlines, lexical ranking is often enough; for terse or ambiguous headlines,
the model must distinguish the specific event.

### Training Data That May Help

Useful training data includes non-overlapping Russian headline-to-article pairs,
news search click or query logs, Russian summarization corpora converted into
retrieval pairs, and hard negatives from same-day or same-topic news clusters.
Because the source corpus is public and ruMTEB uses a test retrieval split,
training should exclude the RiaNewsRetrieval test headlines, qrels, and article
texts that overlap the benchmark.

Models benefit from learning news datelines, quotation patterns, entity aliases,
and event disambiguation in Russian. Dense training should preserve the
headline-to-article asymmetry rather than treating the pair as two interchangeable
sentences.

### Synthetic Data Guidance

For document-to-question generation, use non-evaluation Russian news articles
and generate concise headline-like queries that accurately summarize the article
body. The synthetic query should name the main event, actor, location, and
outcome when those facts are explicit in the article.

For joint generation, create realistic Russian news briefs with agency style,
quotes, dates, and named entities, then write compact headlines for them. Include
hard negatives from articles about the same country, team, official, disaster,
or market indicator but a different event. Do not use evaluation headlines or
positive articles as seeds.
