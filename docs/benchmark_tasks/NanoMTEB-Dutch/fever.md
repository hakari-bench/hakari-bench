# NanoMTEB-Dutch / fever

## Overview

`fever` is the Dutch FEVER retrieval task from BEIR-NL and MTEB-NL. Queries are
Dutch translations of factual claims, and documents are Dutch translated
Wikipedia evidence passages. The task tests whether a model can retrieve
evidence-bearing passages for fact verification, including claims that may be
supported or refuted by the relevant Wikipedia text.

## Details

### What the Original Data Measures

[FEVER: a Large-scale Dataset for Fact Extraction and Verification](https://aclanthology.org/N18-1074/)
introduced FEVER as 185,445 claims generated by altering Wikipedia sentences
and then verifying them against Wikipedia evidence with labels such as
SUPPORTED, REFUTED, and NOT ENOUGH INFO. [BEIR](https://arxiv.org/abs/2104.08663)
uses FEVER as a retrieval task where the claim is the query and evidence-bearing
Wikipedia abstracts are the corpus.

[BEIR-NL](https://aclanthology.org/2025.bucc-1.5/) translates public BEIR
datasets into Dutch and reports native-speaker spot checks over translated
samples. This task is therefore best read as Dutch evidence retrieval over a
translated FEVER test split, not as a natively authored Dutch fact-checking
dataset.

### Observed Data Profile

The Nano split has 200 queries, 10,000 documents, and 233 positive qrel rows.
Most claims have one positive, but 27 queries have multiple positives and the
maximum is 6. Queries average 54.87 characters and are declarative claims.
Documents average 445.71 characters and usually begin with a Wikipedia page
title followed by explanatory passage text.

The sample contains claims about films, medication, mental-health terminology,
English monarchy, and Anne Boleyn. Some positives do not repeat all claim words:
the relevant page may establish a contradiction or provide evidence indirectly,
so models need claim-evidence semantics in addition to entity matching.

### BM25 Difficulty

Using the dataset-provided BM25 candidate column, BM25 reaches nDCG@10 = 0.9221
and hit@10 = 0.9800. This high score reflects the Wikipedia style: claims often
contain names or terms that also occur in the relevant article title or body.

The remaining errors are still meaningful. FEVER relevance is about evidence for
a claim, not only topical similarity. A lexically matching page can be
insufficient when the claim requires a particular relation, date, role, or
negated fact.

### Training Data That May Help

Useful training data includes the official FEVER training data, Dutch or
multilingual claim-evidence retrieval pairs, translated claim verification data
with overlap removed, and Wikipedia passage retrieval pairs. Training should
avoid the upstream FEVER test rows and the translated Dutch queries, positives,
and qrels used in this Nano split.

Because a non-trivial share of queries has multiple positives, listwise or
multi-positive training can help more than reducing every claim to a single
evidence passage.

### Synthetic Data Guidance

For document-to-query generation, use non-evaluation Dutch Wikipedia passages
and generate factual claims that are explicitly supported or contradicted by the
passage. Include entity-near hard negatives from related pages.

For joint generation, create Dutch encyclopedia passages and paired factual
claims with clear evidence spans. The claims should require retrieving evidence,
not memorizing labels, and generated positives should preserve enough context to
verify the claim.
