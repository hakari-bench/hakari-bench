## Overview

`scidocs_nl` is the Dutch SCIDOCS retrieval task from BEIR-NL and MTEB-NL.
Queries are Dutch translated scientific-paper titles, and documents are
translated paper titles and abstracts. The task asks a model to retrieve papers
that are cited by, co-cited with, or otherwise related to the query paper.

## Details

### What the Original Data Measures

[SPECTER: Document-level Representation Learning using Citation-informed Transformers](https://arxiv.org/abs/2004.07180)
introduces SciDocs as a benchmark of seven document-level scientific-paper
tasks, including citation prediction, co-citation, recommendation, and
classification. In BEIR-style retrieval, the query is a scientific paper title
and relevant documents are papers that should be retrieved as related or cited
scientific work.

[MTEB-NL and E5-NL](https://arxiv.org/abs/2509.12340) describes SCIDOCS-NL as a
machine-translated Dutch adaptation from BEIR-NL, where the task is to retrieve
documents that are cited, or should be cited, for a paper given its title.

### Observed Data Profile

The Nano split has 200 queries, 10,000 documents, and 986 positive qrel rows.
Every query has multiple positives: the average is 4.93, the median is 5, and
the maximum is 5. Queries average 77.73 characters and are scientific titles.
Documents average 1,331.57 characters and are abstract-like scientific records.

Examples span energy systems, Bitcoin transaction propagation, waveguide
filters, computational biology software practice, and underwater laser
reconstruction. The positives are often related by citation context rather than
by exact title words, making this a semantic scientific-recommendation task.

### BM25 Difficulty

Using the dataset-provided BM25 candidate column, BM25 reaches nDCG@10 = 0.1335
and hit@10 = 0.4250. This is one of the hardest Dutch tasks in this batch.
Scientific relatedness is often not lexical: a cited paper may share a method,
problem framing, or background area without repeating the query title terms.

The all-multi-positive qrel pattern makes ranking especially important. A model
should return several useful related papers near the top, not just one abstract
with overlapping terminology.

### Training Data That May Help

Useful training data includes non-overlapping citation graph pairs, scientific
paper recommendation datasets, title-to-abstract or title-to-cited-paper pairs,
and multilingual scientific retrieval data with overlap removed. Avoid the
SCIDOCS evaluation title queries, qrels, and positive papers used in this Nano
split.

Multi-positive or listwise training is strongly aligned with this task because
each query has several relevant related papers.

### Synthetic Data Guidance

For document-to-query generation, use non-evaluation scientific abstracts and
generate plausible paper titles that would cite or be related to the document.
The query should be title-like, not a question.

For joint generation, create small clusters of scientific titles and abstracts
around a shared method, dataset, or research problem. Mark several papers as
positive for each title and include hard negatives from the same broad field but
different method or contribution.
