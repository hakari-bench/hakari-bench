## Overview

Climate-FEVER is a fact-checking retrieval task for climate-change claims.
`NanoBEIR-de__NanoClimateFEVER` is the German MNanoBEIR version: each query is
a German translated climate-related claim, and the system must retrieve German
translated Wikipedia evidence passages. The task tests climate-domain
claim/evidence retrieval under long declarative wording, quantities, time
spans, and misinformation-style framing.

## Details

### What the Original Data Measures

[CLIMATE-FEVER: A Dataset for Verification of Real-World Climate
Claims](https://arxiv.org/abs/2012.00614) introduces a dataset for verification
of real-world climate-change claims. The paper adapts FEVER-style verification
to claims collected from the Internet, reports 1,535 verifiable climate claims,
and annotates retrieved evidence candidates as support, refute, or not enough
information. It emphasizes that real climate claims are often subtler than
artificial fact-checking claims because evidence may address only part of a
claim or require numerical and temporal interpretation.

[BEIR: A Heterogeneous Benchmark for Zero-shot Evaluation of Information
Retrieval Models](https://arxiv.org/abs/2104.08663) includes Climate-FEVER in
its fact-checking group, where claims are queries and Wikipedia evidence is the
retrieval target. [MMTEB: Massive Multilingual Text Embedding
Benchmark](https://arxiv.org/abs/2502.13595) provides the multilingual
benchmark context for this compact German Nano split.

### Observed Data Profile

The sampled German Nano task has 50 queries, 3,408 documents, and 148 positive
qrel rows. It is strongly multi-positive: queries average 2.96 positives, 44 of
50 queries have more than one positive, and the maximum is 5. The average query
length is 149.10 characters, and the average document length is 1,767.31
characters.

The inspected queries are long German claims about weather stations, carbon
dioxide, human emissions, Al Gore and climate skepticism, and Holocene warmth.
Positive documents are translated Wikipedia-style pages about cooling towers,
greenhouse gases, carbon dioxide in Earth's atmosphere, Earth, and climate
public discourse. The evidence relation is often indirect, so retrieval is more
than topical climate matching.

### BM25 Difficulty

Using the dataset-provided BM25 candidate column, BM25 reaches nDCG@10 =
0.2371 and hit@10 = 0.5800. Only 10 of 50 queries have a positive ranked first,
and the median first-positive rank is 8. Every query has at least one positive
within the top 100.

This is difficult because long German translated claims contain many generic
climate words, while the relevant passage may use different wording or address
only the needed evidence facet. Strong retrievers should preserve the claim's
subtopic, quantity, and evidential target, then rank multiple evidence passages
highly.

### Training Data That May Help

Useful training data includes non-overlapping Climate-FEVER claim-evidence
pairs, FEVER-style evidence retrieval data, scientific or climate-domain claim
verification data, and German or multilingual claim-to-evidence retrieval pairs.
Training should exclude Climate-FEVER, BEIR, NanoBEIR, or translated records
likely to overlap with these evaluation claims or evidence passages.

### Synthetic Data Guidance

For document-to-query generation, start from non-evaluation climate evidence
passages and generate German declarative claims that can be supported, refuted,
or qualified by the passage. Synthetic data should include quantities, time
periods, causal claims, named institutions, and common climate misinformation
frames. Positives should contain evidence, not merely the same climate topic.
