## Overview

FiQA-2018 is a financial opinion mining and question answering benchmark.
`NanoBEIR-ar__NanoFiQA2018` is the Arabic MNanoBEIR version: each query is an
Arabic personal-finance question, and the system must retrieve Arabic
translated answer posts or answer-like passages that address it. The task tests
retrieval for practical financial advice, account mechanics, investing, debt,
and user-specific decision framing.

## Details

### What the Original Data Measures

[WWW'18 Open Challenge: Financial Opinion Mining and Question
Answering](https://doi.org/10.1145/3184558.3192301) describes FiQA as a Web
Conference 2018 challenge over financial data. Task 2, the source of this
retrieval task, is opinion-based question answering: systems answer natural
language finance questions from a financial knowledge base. The paper reports
that the Opinion QA collection was built from StackExchange posts under the
Investment topic between 2009 and 2017, with 17,110 question-answer pairs for
training and 531 for testing, evaluated with ranking metrics such as nDCG and
MRR.

[BEIR: A Heterogeneous Benchmark for Zero-shot Evaluation of Information
Retrieval Models](https://arxiv.org/abs/2104.08663) includes FiQA-2018 as a
financial question answering retrieval task. [MMTEB: Massive Multilingual Text
Embedding Benchmark](https://arxiv.org/abs/2502.13595) provides the
multilingual benchmark setting for this Arabic version. The Arabic Nano task
keeps the core FiQA behavior: retrieve practical finance answers, not neutral
encyclopedic definitions.

### Observed Data Profile

The sampled Arabic Nano task has 50 queries, 4,598 documents, and 123 positive
qrel rows. It is multi-positive: queries average 2.46 positives, 28 of 50
queries have more than one positive, and the maximum is 15 positives. The
average query length is 53.60 characters, while the average document length is
796.36 characters.

The inspected queries ask practical questions: why countries owe large amounts
of money, whether investment can be done through another person, what happens
to a calendar spread in a non-margin account, whether most investors lose
money, and whether to pay credit-card debt or take an employer 401(k) match.
Positive documents are community-answer style passages with caveats, examples,
numeric assumptions, and product-specific reasoning.

### BM25 Difficulty

Using the dataset-provided BM25 candidate column, BM25 reaches nDCG@10 =
0.2317 and hit@10 = 0.4800. Only 9 of 50 queries have a positive ranked first,
and the median first-positive rank is 15. Every query has a positive within the
top 100, but many positives are far below the top 10.

This is difficult for sparse matching because the query often expresses a
financial decision while the answer explains the reasoning in different words.
Terms such as debt, investing, margin, option exercise, employer match, and
country borrowing can retrieve many superficially related documents. The model
must identify the action being asked about, the financial instrument or account
type, and whether the passage gives usable advice rather than a generic
definition.

### Training Data That May Help

Useful training data includes non-overlapping FiQA question-answer pairs,
financial community QA, personal-finance forum retrieval data, brokerage and
tax FAQ retrieval pairs, and Arabic or multilingual finance QA. Generic QA
data can help with question-answer matching, but domain-specific financial
language and advice structure are important.

Training should exclude FiQA dev/test, BEIR, NanoBEIR, or translated examples
likely to overlap with the evaluation questions and answers. It is also useful
to preserve multi-positive supervision because many questions have several
valid answers.

### Synthetic Data Guidance

For document-to-query generation, start from non-evaluation financial advice
answers and generate Arabic user questions that the answer resolves. Questions
should include account types, jurisdictions, investment products, taxes, debt,
brokerage operations, or retirement accounts.

For joint generation, create Arabic community-style financial answers with
caveats and examples, then generate realistic user questions. Avoid generic
definition pairs unless the answer actually resolves a financial decision.
