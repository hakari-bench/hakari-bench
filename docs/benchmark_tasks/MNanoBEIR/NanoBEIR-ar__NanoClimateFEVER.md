## Overview

Climate-FEVER is a fact-checking retrieval task for climate-change claims.
`NanoBEIR-ar__NanoClimateFEVER` is the Arabic MNanoBEIR version: each query is
an Arabic climate-related claim, and the system must retrieve Arabic translated
Wikipedia evidence passages that support, refute, or otherwise address the
claim. The task tests claim/evidence retrieval under climate terminology,
quantities, time spans, and misinformation-style wording.

## Details

### What the Original Data Measures

[CLIMATE-FEVER: A Dataset for Verification of Real-World Climate
Claims](https://arxiv.org/abs/2012.00614) introduces a dataset for verification
of real-world climate-change claims. The paper adapts FEVER-style verification
to claims collected from the Internet, reports 1,535 verifiable climate claims,
and annotates five retrieved evidence candidates per claim as support, refute,
or not enough information. The paper emphasizes that real climate claims are
subtler than artificial fact-checking claims because evidence can cover only
part of a claim, can require numerical or temporal interpretation, or can
contain conflicting support and refutation.

[BEIR: A Heterogeneous Benchmark for Zero-shot Evaluation of Information
Retrieval Models](https://arxiv.org/abs/2104.08663) includes Climate-FEVER in
its fact-checking group: original claims are queries and evidence is retrieved
from a FEVER-style Wikipedia corpus. [MMTEB: Massive Multilingual Text
Embedding Benchmark](https://arxiv.org/abs/2502.13595) frames the multilingual
benchmark expansion as covering more than 500 quality-controlled tasks across
more than 250 languages and describes retrieval downsampling with hard
negatives to make multilingual evaluation cheaper. This Arabic Nano task should
therefore be read as a compact translated retrieval evaluation of the same
claim-to-evidence behavior, not as a newly annotated Arabic climate dataset.

### Observed Data Profile

The sampled Arabic Nano task has 50 queries, 3,408 documents, and 148 positive
qrel rows. It is strongly multi-positive: queries average 2.96 positives, 44 of
50 queries have more than one positive, and the maximum is 5 positives for one
query. The average query length is 116.76 characters, while the average
document length is 1,342.96 characters.

The Arabic queries are declarative climate claims rather than keyword searches.
The inspected sample includes claims about weather-station placement, carbon
dioxide's role in warming, human emissions and 800,000-year CO2 levels,
skeptical framing around climate change, and Holocene warmth. Positive
documents are translated Wikipedia-style passages about greenhouse gases,
carbon dioxide in the atmosphere, cooling towers, Earth, or climate-related
public discourse. Several positives are indirect evidence pages rather than
passages that simply repeat the claim.

### BM25 Difficulty

Using the dataset-provided BM25 candidate column, BM25 reaches nDCG@10 =
0.2488 and hit@10 = 0.6400. Only 11 of 50 queries have a positive ranked first,
although every query has at least one positive within the top 100. The median
first-positive rank is 6, which makes this one of the harder sparse baselines
in the first Arabic MNanoBEIR batch.

The difficulty comes from long claim wording and evidence indirection. A claim
can contain many climate words but still require evidence about a specific
quantity, phenomenon, or entity. Arabic translation also changes lexical cues:
a retriever must match concepts such as greenhouse gases, human emissions,
warming records, or denialist framing even when the evidence passage uses a
different translated phrasing. Multi-positive ranking matters because one
evidence passage rarely captures all relevant facets.

### Training Data That May Help

Useful non-synthetic training data includes non-overlapping Climate-FEVER
claim-evidence pairs, FEVER-style evidence retrieval data, climate or science
claim verification datasets, and Arabic or multilingual claim-to-evidence
retrieval data. Translation-aligned climate evidence pairs may help because
the benchmark documents and queries are Arabic while the source task was
English.

Training should exclude upstream dev/test examples and BEIR or NanoBEIR records
likely to overlap with the evaluation claims or positive documents. Models
should learn claim-to-evidence retrieval rather than treating the query text as
a true statement.

### Synthetic Data Guidance

For document-to-query generation, start from non-evaluation climate science or
Wikipedia passages and generate Arabic declarative claims that can be supported,
refuted, or qualified by the passage. Good claims should include quantities,
time spans, causal phrasing, named institutions, and common climate
misinformation frames.

For joint generation, create Arabic evidence passages about greenhouse gases,
temperature records, sea level, ice sheets, weather extremes, climate models,
and climate attribution, then generate realistic claims whose positives are the
evidence passages. Include multi-positive examples where several evidence
pages address the same claim from different angles.
