## Overview

NFCorpus is a medical information retrieval dataset built around health and
nutrition information needs linked to research articles. `NanoBEIR-de__NanoNFCorpus`
is the German MNanoBEIR version: short German translated lay health queries must
retrieve German translated medical or biomedical documents. The task stresses
domain terminology, consumer-health phrasing, and many relevant documents per
query.

## Details

### What the Original Data Measures

[A Full-Text Learning to Rank Dataset for Medical Information
Retrieval](https://www.cl.uni-heidelberg.de/~riezler/publications/papers/ECIR2016.pdf)
describes NFCorpus as a learning-to-rank dataset whose queries come from
NutritionFacts.org health topics, while relevance links are derived from direct
and indirect links to PubMed or medical articles at multiple relevance levels.
The paper highlights the lexical gap between lay health queries and medical
literature.

[BEIR: A Heterogeneous Benchmark for Zero-shot Evaluation of Information
Retrieval Models](https://arxiv.org/abs/2104.08663) includes NFCorpus as a
bio-medical retrieval task. [MMTEB: Massive Multilingual Text Embedding
Benchmark](https://arxiv.org/abs/2502.13595) provides the multilingual context
for this German Nano split.

### Observed Data Profile

The sampled German Nano task has 50 queries, 2,953 documents, and 1,651 positive
qrel rows. Queries average 33.02 positives, with a range from 1 to 100 positives;
47 of 50 queries are multi-positive. The average query length is 29.14
characters, and the average document length is 1,731.88 characters.

The inspected queries include lay or topic-like health needs such as chicken
nuggets, meat detox and cola, Atkins diet, adenovirus 36, and probiotics for
cold prevention. Documents are long translated biomedical abstracts or article
summaries with technical language and study-design details.

### BM25 Difficulty

Using the dataset-provided BM25 candidate column, BM25 reaches nDCG@10 =
0.2753 and hit@10 = 0.6400. BM25 ranks at least one positive first for 20
queries, but the median first-positive rank is 4.5 and nDCG remains low because
there are often many relevant documents to rank.

This task is difficult because consumer-style queries can be short and vague
while positives use biomedical vocabulary. Even when BM25 finds one related
article, ranking many relevant abstracts near the top requires matching health
concepts, interventions, mechanisms, and outcomes beyond exact terms.

### Training Data That May Help

Useful training data includes non-overlapping biomedical information retrieval
data, consumer-health question to biomedical abstract pairs, PubMed relevance
ranking data, German or multilingual medical retrieval supervision, and
terminology-rich paraphrase data.

Training should avoid NFCorpus, BEIR, NanoBEIR, or translated NutritionFacts
records that overlap with these evaluation queries or linked articles.

### Synthetic Data Guidance

For document-to-query generation, start from non-evaluation biomedical abstracts
and generate short German lay questions or topic labels. Synthetic queries
should deliberately bridge consumer vocabulary to medical mechanisms, dietary
interventions, disease names, and outcomes.

For joint generation, create clusters of related biomedical passages so that
multi-positive training teaches a model to rank several valid evidence articles
for the same health topic.
