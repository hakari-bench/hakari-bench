## Overview

NFCorpus is a medical information retrieval dataset linking lay health and
nutrition topics to scientific medical documents. `NanoBEIR-ar__NanoNFCorpus`
is the Arabic MNanoBEIR version: short Arabic health-topic queries must
retrieve Arabic translated biomedical abstracts and medically oriented
documents. The task tests the consumer-health to scientific-language retrieval
gap.

## Details

### What the Original Data Measures

[A Full-Text Learning to Rank Dataset for Medical Information
Retrieval](https://www.cl.uni-heidelberg.de/~riezler/publications/papers/ECIR2016.pdf)
introduces NFCorpus as a medical learning-to-rank dataset. The paper explains
that queries come from NutritionFacts.org health topics written for lay readers,
while documents are mostly PubMed and PMC titles and abstracts. Relevance links
are extracted from direct and indirect links between NutritionFacts pages and
medical articles, producing a retrieval task that bridges consumer health
language and technical biomedical evidence.

[BEIR: A Heterogeneous Benchmark for Zero-shot Evaluation of Information
Retrieval Models](https://arxiv.org/abs/2104.08663) includes NFCorpus in its
biomedical information retrieval group. [MMTEB: Massive Multilingual Text
Embedding Benchmark](https://arxiv.org/abs/2502.13595) gives the multilingual
benchmark setting for this Arabic Nano version. The Arabic task keeps the
NFCorpus evidence-retrieval character: short health terms and titles are
matched to scientific abstracts, not consumer-facing answers.

### Observed Data Profile

The sampled Arabic Nano task has 50 queries, 2,953 documents, and 1,651
positive qrel rows. It is highly multi-positive: queries average 33.02
positives, 47 of 50 queries have more than one positive, and one query has 100
positives. The average query length is only 22.30 characters, while the average
document length is 1,408.18 characters.

The inspected queries are short NutritionFacts-style health topics, including
meat purge and cola, industrial toxins, saturated fat, antinutrients, and
forbidden food. Positive documents are translated biomedical abstracts or
technical summaries with study objectives, methods, outcomes, biomarkers, and
diet or disease terminology. The relevance relation is often topical and
evidence-based rather than a direct answer sentence.

### BM25 Difficulty

Using the dataset-provided BM25 candidate column, BM25 reaches nDCG@10 =
0.2440 and hit@10 = 0.5200. BM25 ranks a positive first for 21 of 50 queries,
and the median first-positive rank is 9. Every query has a positive within the
top 100, but the low nDCG shows that many relevant abstracts are not ranked
near the top.

This task is hard for sparse matching because a short lay query can map to many
scientific documents whose wording differs from the query. For example, a query
about antinutrients can retrieve a technical document about phosphorus additives
in chicken products, and a broad phrase like saturated fat can have many
related biomedical positives. Strong retrievers should learn consumer-health to
biomedical associations and rank many relevant evidence documents, not just one
lexically close abstract.

### Training Data That May Help

Useful training data includes the non-overlapping NFCorpus train split,
consumer health question to PubMed evidence pairs, BioASQ-style
question-to-article supervision, biomedical abstract retrieval data, and Arabic
or multilingual medical retrieval pairs. Multi-positive objectives are
important because the task contains many positives per query.

Training should exclude upstream dev/test data and NutritionFacts, NFCorpus,
BEIR, or NanoBEIR records likely to overlap with these evaluation topics or
documents.

### Synthetic Data Guidance

For document-to-query generation, use non-evaluation biomedical abstracts and
generate short Arabic consumer-health topics, food names, drug names, disease
phrases, or NutritionFacts-style titles that the abstract supports.

For joint generation, create Arabic biomedical abstract-style passages with
population, intervention or exposure, outcome, and measurements, then pair them
with broad lay health topics. Include multiple positives for the same topic and
avoid generic medical neighbors that are not evidence for the query.
