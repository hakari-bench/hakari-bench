## Overview

The MIRACL paper defines French retrieval as same-language ad hoc search over
French Wikipedia passages, with natural questions and human relevance labels.
This Nano split keeps that passage-retrieval setting in a compact corpus. The
questions are ordinary French information needs beginning with forms such as
`Qui`, `Quel`, `Quand`, `Comment`, `Combien`, `Pourquoi`, and `Où`, so the
retriever must select the passage that carries the requested fact across people,
places, science, music, politics, history, mathematics, demographics, food, and
definitions.

## Details

### What the Original Data Measures

[MIRACL: A Multilingual Retrieval Dataset Covering 18 Diverse
Languages](https://aclanthology.org/2023.tacl-1.63/) describes MIRACL as a
monolingual ad hoc retrieval benchmark over Wikipedia passages. French queries
retrieve French passages, so the task evaluates same-language retrieval rather
than translation or cross-lingual matching. The paper emphasizes natural
questions, natural passages, and human relevance labels.

French belongs to MIRACL's "new known languages" group. The earlier MIRACL
overview explains that Hindi, Spanish, French, Farsi, and Chinese were added
beyond Mr. TyDi/TyDi QA, and that all data for those languages was generated
from scratch. This means the French split is a MIRACL-created French Wikipedia
retrieval collection, not a denser relabeling of an older Mr. TyDi split.

The paper's annotation workflow is important for interpretation: annotators
generated questions from Wikipedia prompts, then judged candidate passages
returned by an ensemble including BM25, mDPR, and mColBERT. The TACL paper also
notes that BM25 has relatively low effectiveness in French and Chinese compared
with many other MIRACL languages, while hybrid BM25+mDPR is a strong zero-shot
baseline. The French Nano sample below reflects that lexical retrieval is often
not enough to rank the evidence passage highly.

### Observed Data Profile

The sampled Nano task has 200 queries, 1,777 documents, and 200 positive qrel
rows. Every query has exactly one positive passage. Queries average 43.26
characters and are ordinary French questions. Common openings include `Qui`,
`Quels`, `Quelles`, `Quel`, `Quand`, `Quelle`, `Comment`, `Combien`,
`Pourquoi`, `Où`, and `Qu’est-ce`.

Documents average 556.73 characters and are French Wikipedia passages that
usually begin with the article title. The observed positives cover musical
soloists, biomedical data management, French commune population evidence,
American independence history, mathematical axioms, Japanese geography, branches
of chemistry, the Orinoco river, country music, French city population, Belgian
sports, gardening, coffee production, the North African campaign, electricity
generation, New York demography, and flower morphology.

The task is especially sensitive to relation and passage selection. A query can
contain a clear entity or topic, but the positive may be a nearby passage that
answers the exact relation rather than the most obvious page title. For example,
a question about the population of Charbonnières is paired with a
Corbières-en-Provence passage in the sampled data, and a question about
biological data collection is paired with a biomedical data-manager passage.
Readers should treat these pages as passage-retrieval evidence tasks over the
sampled corpus, not as standalone fact sheets.

### BM25 Difficulty

Using the dataset-provided BM25 candidate column, BM25 reaches nDCG@10 = 0.3034
and hit@10 = 0.5700 on this Nano split. BM25 places only 17 of 200 positives at
rank 1 and 114 of 200 positives in the top 10. This is one of the harder
NanoMIRACL splits inspected so far for sparse retrieval.

The misses show several patterns. For "Où se trouve le fleuve Orénoque?", BM25
finds an Orénoque song passage and unrelated pages before the positive river
passage. For "Quelles sont les branches de la chimie?", it ranks pages about
Celtic languages and inorganic chemistry while the positive passage is a broader
chemist page. For "Quel pays est le plus grand fabricant de café?", BM25
retrieves coffee-terroir and Sri Lanka coffee-cultivation passages ahead of the
general coffee passage that lists major producing regions. For "Quelle est la
morphologie d’une fleur?", linguistic morphology pages outrank the positive
flower-morphology passage. These examples show that French lexical overlap can
retrieve a plausible topic while missing the passage that answers the specific
question.

Because the split is single-positive, hit@10 indicates whether the labeled
evidence passage appears at all, and here BM25 misses 86 queries from the top
10. nDCG@10 adds a ranking signal for positives that are present but buried. A
strong model should improve relation-aware ranking, sense disambiguation, and
handling of French question forms while preserving exact entity matching.

### Training Data That May Help

Non-overlapping French MIRACL training data is the first source to inspect.
Because this task is MIRACL-derived, upstream development or test queries, qrels,
and positive passages likely to overlap with NanoMIRACL should preferably be
excluded from training. Other useful data includes French Wikipedia
question-to-passage retrieval pairs, French open-domain QA evidence datasets,
and French entity-attribute supervision for places, dates, counts, professions,
definitions, causes, and classifications.

Training should emphasize evidence passage retrieval rather than only answer
generation. The model needs to rank the passage that expresses the requested
relation above related passages sharing French morphology, topic words, or
entity aliases.

### Synthetic Data Guidance

For document-to-question generation, use non-evaluation French Wikipedia-style
passages and generate French questions grounded in one selected passage. Useful
forms include `Qui`, `Quel`, `Quelle`, `Quels`, `Quelles`, `Quand`, `Où`,
`Comment`, `Combien`, `Pourquoi`, and `Qu’est-ce`, with named entities,
locations, counts, roles, definitions, causes, and scientific terms.

For joint document-and-question generation, create French encyclopedia-style
passages with titles, aliases, demographic facts, dates, roles, lists, and
definition sentences, then generate answerable French questions. Do not seed
generation with Nano evaluation queries or positive passages. Include related
but non-answering distractors, especially homonymous terms such as morphology in
linguistics versus biology or location questions where a nearby named entity is
not enough.
